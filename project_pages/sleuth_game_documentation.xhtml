Sleuth Game

Production Blog

Introduction

For my design project I chose to plan, create designs and make assets for a high-poly 3D cooperative multiplayer computer game called Sleuth. For my deliverables I ended up creating character designs, the modelled, textured and rendered exterior environment, and designs for the user interface.

Synopsis

Sleuth takes place in the 1920s, in the French Chateau of a wealthy family. A murder has taken place in the mansion, and the players (up to five) have to figure out who the culprit is. However, the culprit still lurks around the building, and will attack the investigative team in order to stop them solving the case.

Each of the playable characters has their own unique special ability that can be used to help them solve the case or fight off the attacker.

The game is a deductive horror/murder mystery that requires the players to cooperate to solve the case.

For the pre-production, we came up with our initial idea, created sketches, and then iterative prototypes until we decided on a final design.

My initial sketches of mansions led my lecturer to suggest a make the setting a French Chateau, as that was the style I had sketched a lot of.

I then made several prototypes with different combinations of character time periods, mansions and font styles.

From these prototypes, I selected and refined the three I thought would work the best, and then we were to choose between the final three for our project.

From the final prototypes, I decided to continue working on refined prototype 1.

Break Down

Planning the Environment:

For the second part of the project, I began creating a floorplan of the building to use as a blueprint for blocking in Autodesk Maya. I used a rectangle as a size reference for a person to try and make the rooms reasonably sized compared to one another. I looked into different French Chateaus and rooms they often had and planned the layout from there. I planned the building with three floors, the additional floor being in the attic. Finally, I imported the finalised floor plan into an Autodesk Maya scene to begin the modelling.

Blocking the Environment:

I used the imported floor plans to scale polygon primitives to appropriate sizes and shapes. I made sure to be careful with my edge loops to avoid ngons from the start of the modelling process to prevent issues down the line. At this stage I blocked out the main building, interior walls, roof and front stairs.

While blocking the interior walls I realised the drastically varying height of the mansion roof would cause issues with the character fitting through doors and under roofs in the attic, so I decided to leave out the third floor.

Low Poly Modelling:

After blocking the environment, I added more detail to make low poly models. This included adding windows and doors as well as their frames to the model and adding a courtyard area with a water feature and benches. If elements were repeated I only modelled them once and duplicated them later after smoothing. I realised for rendering it would look better if I had a textured landscape around the building as well, so I created a simple low poly mesh and added height variation for realism to create a landscape for the building. I realised at this stage I was not going to have time to model any of the interior as I had originally hoped to do so I focused on creating a high quality exterior.

UV Unwrapping and Smooth Preparation:

After creating the low poly models, I added basic phong E Maya materials and used the UV editor to UV unwrap all of them, prioritising minimising warping as it is an architectural object and it can cause issues with brick patterns. I tried to hide seams where possible, and where not possible placed them in areas that would be seen by players less often. After UV unwrapping each model I used smooth preview to determine where supporting edge loops were needed and added them as required, checking to ensure this did not create any ngons.

Smoothing:

Next I applied the smoothing, set to 1 or 2 depending on the size and complexity of the model. I left the main building frame and windows without smoothing as they did not have any visible curved surfaces and this would prevent unnecessary detail causing the application to run slower. After smoothing I duplicated the components that were repeated in the scene, positioned them as needed, and then combined most of the components, leaving the landscape separate. I also left each of the doors as separate meshes to ensure they could be functional in the game. I laid out the UVs for the combined objects, however, due to the large polygon count and size of the main building, I was limited to 2048 for the resolution and 4 UDIMs, which restricted the amount of detail visible in the applied textures. If I were to continue with this project I would likely need to invest in a computer with more processing power.

Exporting and Texturing:

I exported my meshes as fbx files and brought them into Adobe Substance Painter for texturing. I used 4096 resolution for the textures in Substance Painter but still found the amount of texture detail I could achieve to be very limited due to the small number of UDIMs for such a large mesh. I discovered at this point I had ended up with some strange geometry on the front of the building mesh, so I fixed this in Maya and reimported it to Substance Painter.

I did research into which textures would be most appropriate for a 1920s French Chateau, finding that slate roof tiles and parquet floors were popular (Chateau De Pleuville n.d., para. 3; DaVinci Roofscapes 2022, para. 1; Livingstone 2019, para. 7), outer walls were often limestone and inner walls filled with limestone rubble, mud, lime mortar or plaster (Chateau De Pleuville n.d., para. 1). Other materials used often included reinforced concrete, glass and steel (Bertolazzi 2013, p. 1). For the textures, I used some of the default textures and some additional textures downloaded from Adobe's Substance 3D Assets page to find those that matched closely to the materials I had researched. For the roof I made a custom stencil to achieve the roof tile look that I wanted. I initially created a stencil for long rectangular tiles, then created one for tiles with beveled corners, which I ended up using because I thought the more rounded look suited the French Chateau style better. I also created a custom stencil for the doorknobs in a 1920s French Chateau style, however, due to the limited resolution this was not very clearly visible in the final rendered result.

Rendering:

Next I moved on to the rendering stage, which was by far the most problematic of all the stages of my project. I had initially planned to texture using Autodesk Arnold in Maya, as I had done my modelling in Maya and found that usually my computer ran better with Maya than Unreal Engine. However, I encountered a number of issues in the Arnold settings that were not compatible with my computer such as issues with the type of Denoiser and necessary hardware. After I finally got it to work, it was taking far too long to render the images that I needed, so I decided to try Unreal Engine again since I had used it in the past.

Using Unreal Engine I was easily able to set up the materials. From here, I used level sequences to render images and videos from the textured model, using a tutorial video (DrawWithNightBuzzer 2023) for instructions. However, I encountered another hardware problem, as I was not able to use path tracing on my computer. I researched how I would need to change the settings they used in the video to work for my computer. I was then able to render images and videos relatively quickly at 1920 x 1080p.

Improving Character Design Presentation:

While waiting for the various applications and software to load, I improved the presentation of the character designs on my website by adding a simple picture frame around the designs and adding a more detailed description about them.

User Interface Designs:

Finally, I used some of the rendered images of the environment to make designs for the user interface, specifically the title screen, in-game maps and role-based screen layout for different characters. I had some trouble deciding on fonts for the title screen and ended up using one of the fonts from a different prototype as I thought it suited the game better. I used my previous floor plans to make a map screen that would overlay the main game to help players navigate the building. I used dark, desaturated colours and a dark red to enhance the horror themes.

I created designs for the main user interface of the game, with different variations for each character. Each character's special ability would appear differently on the screen, and would also work differently once activated, so I created different versions of the interface design for each character.

Finally, I presented all of the visuals for the deliverables on my website for potential employers to view.

Contributions

I completed my project individually. This included planning the game, designing the characters, modelling, texturing and rendering the exterior environment and designing the user interface. I received feedback from my lecturer in the weekly classes over the two trimester duration of the task and got some feedback from my classmates.

Responding to Feedback

I received a significant amount of feedback over the course of the project, mostly from my lecturer. I implemented a significant amount of the feedback but declined some because I didn't think it was suitable.

My lecturer suggested adding an introduction clip for the mystery at the start of the game, such as a news report. I thought this was a good idea but unfortunately didn't have enough time to implement it, however, I do think it would be included in the final version for release.

I was initially undecided on whether the attacker was a member of the five characters or an external non-player character. In my feedback from my lecturer it was suggested I needed to decide this as it would have a significant impact on gameplay. I ended up deciding the attacker would be a non-player character, as I had originally intended to create a cooperative multiplayer game to encourage people to collaborate and work together to solve the mystery, and I think having a player be the attacker would hinder this goal.

It was suggested by my lecturer and classmate Harry that I make the game a combination of online and in person gameplay. I ended up deciding not to implement this feedback as I think it is more important for the game to be accessible to players at all different locations, while in person gameplay would require players to be able to meet up in real life.

I was told by my lecturer to expand on the characters, and later to give them names to give them more personality. I did want to keep the characters without too much backstory to allow the players to project their own personality onto the characters, however, I do think there is a benefit to making the characters more distinct, so I did end up naming them. I researched popular French first and surnames from the 1920s (Behind the Name 2022; Forebears n.d.) and used those to name the characters appropriately for the setting.

I was also given feedback about the expandability of the game concept; that is, the potential for sequels or a movie adaptation. I do think that is very much possible with such a broad concept. It was suggested that some of the prototypes of the characters in different time periods could be implemented as additional games in the franchise, which I think is a good idea.

It was suggested that I actually include the floor plans I had made in game as a map for players. I thought this was an excellent suggestion and used them to make a design for a map screen.

It was also suggested I try to ensure the environment was fitting for the 1920s setting. While there weren't a lot of objects as I only modelled the exterior, I did research the materials used during that time period for accurate texturing.

When I was struggling to decide on fonts, I asked a friend for their opinion and ended up using the font they chose, Another Danger (The Branded Quotes 2016), from the two I presented.

Reflection

Overall I am happy with the results of my design project. I think my main struggles during this project were time management and rendering the models. I had trouble completing the amount of work I hoped to due to struggles with managing my workload, as I would have liked to have modelled an interior room as well, however, I am happy with the quality of the deliverables I did accomplish.

I think initially I was overambitious in what I hoped to achieve as I thought I would be able to complete the interior and exterior. However, I was happy with the outcome of the exterior, and with the type of project I created I had room for flexibility in how much would be completed in the two trimesters.

I had a lot of trouble because of the limitations of my computer, so if I were to fully implement this project I would need stronger hardware. I found that the slow loading times and poor texture rendering in real time made it very difficult to assess if the texture looked right or not. I also had significant difficulty with rendering, in part due to the hardware limitations. I think in hindsight I should have rendered in Unreal Engine from the start because I am familiar with it and that is likely where the game would be implemented anyway.

I found getting consistent feedback throughout the trimesters helpful and it led to the development of several ideas I hadn't considered originally.

I think this project successfully demonstrates my capabilities in 3D modelling, texturing and rendering, as well as game design in general. I have demonstrated the use of several different industry-standard software products, including Autodesk Maya, Unreal Engine, Adobe Substance Painter and Adobe Illustrator, which shows my technical proficiency, while the concept development demonstrates my creativity and ability to come up with several different ideas for one project.
